Service of process is the foundation of your case. Get one step wrong under Florida law and a court can quash the serve, erasing weeks of progress and putting your deadlines at risk. This guide explains what Florida Statutes Chapter 48 actually requires for valid service of process — in plain English, for attorneys and self-represented litigants alike, so a technicality never stalls your case.

This article is general information, not legal advice. For advice about your specific case, talk to a Florida attorney.

Who can legally serve process in Florida

Under Chapter 48, service of process in Florida may be carried out by the county sheriff or by a private process server who is either certified (in circuits with certification programs) or specially appointed by the court or sheriff. A random adult cannot serve your papers — and neither can you. Parties to the case are prohibited from serving their own documents, no exceptions.

This is the first thing to verify when hiring a Florida process server: are they authorized in the county where service will happen? A serve performed by an unauthorized person is void, no matter how flawlessly it was executed. Because authorization is county-specific, a server working across metros like Tampa, Miami, Orlando, Jacksonville, St. Petersburg, and Fort Lauderdale must be properly certified or appointed in each circuit where they deliver documents.

Personal service: the gold standard

Personal service — physically delivering the documents to the named individual — is the method Chapter 48 prefers, and the one least likely to be challenged. The server must identify the person, announce the nature of the documents, and leave the papers with them. Refusing to accept the summons does not defeat service: once the person is identified and informed, documents left in their presence complete the serve. That is why a return of service documenting who, when, and where matters as much as the delivery itself.

Substitute service rules

When the defendant cannot be personally reached, Florida permits substitute service: leaving the documents at the person's usual place of abode with any resident aged 15 or older, then informing that resident of the contents. Some documents — notably injunctions for protection — require personal service and cannot be substituted, so identifying the correct method up front protects the serve.

Substitute service on a spouse is allowed anywhere in the county under certain conditions, and service on employers, gated communities, and corporate registered agents each carries its own subsections. This is where experienced Florida process servers earn their fee — knowing which variation of Chapter 48 applies, whether the defendant is in a Central Florida subdivision or a South Florida high-rise.

Days, times & restrictions on service in Florida

Florida generally prohibits service on Sundays, and a serve made on Sunday is voidable. Saturday and evening serves, by contrast, are fully lawful — and often the most effective way to reach a hard-to-find defendant at home. There is no rule against serving someone at work, at the gym, or in a parking lot: if the person is located and identified, they can be served anywhere in the county.

Mistakes that get service quashed in Florida courts

The most common grounds for quashing service of process in Florida courts are: an unauthorized server, a substitute serve on someone under 15 or on a non-resident, a defective return of service missing required details, serving the wrong person of a similar name, and Sunday service. Florida Process Serving Laws FAQ

Can I serve papers myself in Florida?

No. Parties to a case cannot serve their own process under Florida law. Service must be performed by the county sheriff or by a certified or court-appointed private process server authorized in that county.

Is a serve valid if the person refuses to take the papers?

Generally yes. If the server identifies the person and announces the documents, leaving them in that person's presence completes service. Walking away or refusing to touch the papers does not undo a valid serve.

What happens if service is quashed in a Florida court?

The case does not end, but you must re-serve correctly — losing time and possibly missing deadlines. Getting valid service the first time is far cheaper than fixing it later, which is why disciplined, documented service matters.

Does Chapter 48 apply in every Florida county?

Yes. Florida Statutes Chapter 48 governs service of process statewide, across all 67 Florida counties — from Tampa and St. Petersburg to Miami, Fort Lauderdale, Orlando, and Jacksonville. What varies by circuit is the certification or appointment process for private servers, not the underlying rules for a valid serve.

What if the defendant is evasive or hard to find?

An evasive defendant does not put your case out of reach. Skip tracing locates a current address, and Florida's substitute-service and repeated-attempt rules provide lawful paths to complete the serve. A documented pattern of attempts also supports a motion for alternate service if one becomes necessary.
