If you've never had to serve someone before, Florida's service-of-process rules can feel like a trap: get one step wrong and a court can quash service, undoing weeks of progress. This guide walks through what Florida Statutes Chapter 48 actually requires — in plain English.

This article is general information, not legal advice. For advice about your case, talk to a Florida attorney.

Who can legally serve process in Florida

Florida allows service by the county sheriff or by a private process server who is either certified (in circuits with certification programs) or specially appointed by the court or sheriff. A random adult cannot serve your papers, and neither can you — parties to the case are prohibited from serving their own documents.

This is the first thing to verify when hiring a process server: are they authorized in the county where service will happen? A serve by an unauthorized person is void, no matter how perfectly it was performed.

Personal service: the gold standard

Personal service — physically delivering the documents to the named person — is the standard Chapter 48 prefers. The server must identify the person, announce the nature of the documents, and leave them with the individual. Refusing to take the papers doesn't defeat service: if the person is identified and informed, documents left in their presence complete the serve.

Substitute service rules

When the defendant can't be personally reached, Florida permits substitute service: leaving documents at the person's usual place of abode with any resident aged 15 or older, and informing that person of the contents. Some documents — notably injunctions for protection — require personal service and cannot be substituted.

Substitute service on a spouse is allowed anywhere in the county under certain conditions; service on employers, gated communities, and corporate agents each have their own subsections. This is where experienced servers earn their fee — knowing which variation applies.

Days, times & restrictions

Florida generally prohibits service on Sundays, and a serve made on Sunday is voidable. Saturday and evening serves are fully lawful — and often the most effective. There's no rule against serving someone at work, at the gym, or in a parking lot: if the person is found, they can be served.

Mistakes that get service quashed

The most common grounds for quashing service in Florida courts: an unauthorized server, a substitute serve on someone under 15 or a non-resident, a defective return of service missing required details, serving the wrong person of a similar name, and Sunday service. Every one of these is preventable with process discipline — which is why our affidavits record who, when, where, how, and GPS-verified proof on every attempt.

Key takeaway

Valid service is about the details: right server, right recipient, right day, right paperwork. Hire a licensed professional and the details are their problem, not yours.

FAQ

Can I serve papers myself in Florida?

No — parties to a case cannot serve their own process. Use the sheriff or a certified/appointed private process server.

Is a serve valid if the person refuses to take the papers?

Generally yes. If the server identifies the person and announces the documents, leaving them in the person's presence completes service — running away doesn't undo it.

What happens if service is quashed?

The case doesn't end, but you must re-serve correctly — losing time and possibly deadlines. Getting it right the first time is far cheaper.
