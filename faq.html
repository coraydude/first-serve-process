Process Serving Questions, Answered

Straight answers about Florida service of process, deadlines, costs, affidavits, and what to do when a defendant can't be found.

Process serving basics

Who can serve legal papers in Florida?

A sheriff, or a certified/court-appointed process server under Florida Statutes Chapter 48. All First Serve servers hold the appointments required in the circuits where they work.

Can papers be left with someone else?

Yes — Florida permits substitute service at the defendant's usual place of abode with a resident who is 15 or older, with proper disclosure. Some documents (like injunctions) require personal service; we'll flag those.

Can you serve someone at work?

Yes. Florida law allows service wherever the person is found, including workplaces. We handle workplace serves discreetly and professionally.

What happens after the serve?

You receive a notarized affidavit (return) of service. We can also file it with the court for you and store GPS/photo evidence in case service is ever contested.

Deadlines & rush service

How fast is standard service?

First attempts within 24–48 hours of receiving documents, with multiple attempts at varied times, including evenings and Saturdays.

What counts as a rush?

Any job where you tell us the deadline drives the schedule — we dispatch to the nearest server for attempts within hours. See Rush Process Serving.

Can you really serve the same day?

Yes — documents in by 2pm are attempted that calendar day in major Florida metros. See Same-Day Service.

Do you serve on weekends?

Saturdays, yes. Florida law generally prohibits service on Sundays, and certain documents have additional day restrictions — we manage those rules for you.

Costs & quotes

How much does a process server cost in Florida?

Pricing depends on the county, urgency, and number of addresses. We quote a flat fee up front — covering multiple attempts — before you commit. Request a quote for exact pricing.

Are there hidden fees?

No. The quoted flat fee covers standard attempts, the notarized affidavit, and status updates. Extras like skip tracing, stakeouts, or filing are always priced before we proceed.

Do I pay if service fails?

The service fee covers the attempt work performed. If an address is bad, we tell you quickly and credit our diligence toward next steps like skip tracing wherever possible.

Affidavits & court rules

What proof of service will I receive?

A notarized affidavit of service stating who was served, when, where, and how — formatted to the receiving court's requirements, delivered digitally, with the original available on request.

Will your affidavit hold up if challenged?

Yes — every attempt carries GPS coordinates, timestamps, and photos, and our servers testify when needed. Contested-service hearings are rare; lost ones are rarer.

Can you file the affidavit with the court?

Yes — add court filing and returns are filed automatically as soon as they're notarized.

Skip tracing

What if I don't have a current address?

Our licensed skip tracing team locates current residences and workplaces, usually within 24–72 hours, then serves immediately on the verified address.

Is skip tracing legal?

Yes — for permissible purposes like service of process, using lawful data sources, compliant with the FCRA, GLBA, and DPPA.

What if the person can't be found at all?

You receive a documented no-locate report of everything checked — the due-diligence record courts expect before authorizing alternative service such as service by publication.
