Florida Process Serving FAQ & Service of Process Answers

Clear, plain-English answers about Florida service of process — deadlines, costs, notarized affidavits, and what to do when a defendant is evading service — from experienced process servers covering all 67 Florida counties.

Service of process basics under Florida Statutes Chapter 48

Who can serve legal papers in Florida?

Service of process in Florida is performed by a sheriff or by a certified, court-appointed process server under Florida Statutes Chapter 48. Every First Serve server holds the appointments required in the circuits where they work, so your summons, subpoena, or complaint is served by someone the court recognizes across Tampa, Orlando, Miami, Jacksonville, and every Florida county.

Can legal papers be left with someone else?

Yes. Florida permits substitute service at the defendant's usual place of abode with a co-resident who is 15 or older, provided the required disclosures are made. Certain documents — injunctions, for example — demand personal service on the individual, and we flag those before we attempt so your service holds up.

Can you serve someone at their workplace?

Yes. Florida law allows service of process wherever the person is lawfully found, including a place of employment. Our Florida process servers handle workplace serves discreetly and professionally, so the defendant is served without unnecessary disruption or grounds to challenge the return.

What happens after the defendant is served?

You receive a notarized affidavit — the return of service — documenting who was served, when, where, and how. We can also file the return with the court on your behalf and retain GPS-verified, time-stamped evidence of every attempt in case service is ever contested.

Deadlines, rush & same-day process service in Florida

How fast is standard service of process?

First attempts are made within 24–48 hours of receiving your documents, with multiple follow-up attempts at varied times — including evenings and Saturdays — to catch a defendant who keeps an unpredictable schedule.

What counts as a rush serve?

Any job where a filing deadline or hearing date drives the schedule. Tell us the deadline and we dispatch to the nearest available server for attempts within hours across Florida's major metros. See Rush Process Serving.

Can you really serve the same day in Florida?

Yes. Documents received by 2pm are attempted that same calendar day in Tampa, Orlando, Miami, St. Petersburg, Fort Lauderdale, Jacksonville, and other major Florida metros. See Same-Day Service.

Do you serve legal documents on weekends?

Saturdays, yes. Florida law generally prohibits service on Sundays, and specific document types carry additional day and time restrictions — we track and manage those rules for you so nothing gets quashed on a technicality.

Do I pay if service fails?

Affidavits of service & court-compliant proof

What proof of service will I receive?

A notarized affidavit of service stating who was served, when, where, and how — formatted to the receiving court's requirements, delivered digitally, with the original available on request. It's court-ready proof designed to keep your case moving instead of stalling on defective service.

Will your affidavit hold up if service is challenged?

Yes. Every attempt is backed by GPS coordinates, timestamps, and photos, and our servers are available to testify if a return is contested. That evidence trail is exactly what a Florida court expects when the sufficiency of service is questioned.

How do I get the affidavit filed with the court?

We deliver your notarized return of service formatted to the receiving court's requirements and ready to e-file with the clerk, so your deadline is met without a second trip to the courthouse.

Skip tracing & serving evasive defendants in Florida

What if I don't have a current address for the defendant?

Our licensed skip tracing team locates current residences and workplaces — usually within 24–72 hours — then serves immediately on the verified address. It's the fastest path to serving an evasive defendant anywhere in Florida.

Is skip tracing legal?

Yes — for permissible purposes such as service of process, using lawful data sources in compliance with the FCRA, GLBA, and DPPA. Every trace we run supports a legitimate legal matter.

What if the person still can't be found?

You receive a documented no-locate report detailing everything checked — the diligent-search record a Florida court expects before authorizing alternative service, such as service by publication.

How do you serve a defendant who is actively evading service?

We combine varied-hour attempts, skip tracing, and — when the case warrants it — stakeout services to reach a defendant who is dodging service. Every attempt is logged with GPS and timestamps, building the diligence record that supports substitute or court-ordered alternative service if personal service can't be completed.

Yes. Beyond all 67 Florida counties, we coordinate nationwide process serving through a vetted partner network reaching all 50 states, so your out-of-state defendants are served under the same court-compliant standards.
