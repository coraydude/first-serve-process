Evasion is a strategy. So is ours.

Some defendants learn the sound of a process server's knock: they stop answering the door, park around the corner, and let mail pile up. Standard repeat attempts waste your money on people like this — surveillance doesn't.

A stakeout begins with intelligence: our skip trace data on vehicles, work schedules, and routines. Servers then hold position at the residence or workplace, confirm the subject's presence, and serve at the moment of contact — commonly at arrival or departure.

If a subject still can't be served, our detailed surveillance logs give your attorney exactly what Florida courts want to see before authorizing substitute or constructive service.

Stakeout questions

How long does a typical stakeout last?

Most are 2–4 hour sessions targeted at high-probability windows (early morning departures, evening arrivals). You set the budget cap; we recommend windows based on trace data.

Is surveillance for service of process legal?

Yes — observing from public vantage points to effect lawful service is permitted. Our servers follow Florida law and avoid trespass, harassment, and pretext.

What if the subject never appears?

You receive the full surveillance log regardless. Combined with prior attempt records, it's the due-diligence evidence courts expect before authorizing substitute service.

Can you serve someone in their car?

Yes. Florida permits personal service wherever the person is found, including vehicle stops in driveways or parking lots, handled safely and professionally.
